Charles Darwin

History's most famous biologist

Charles Darwin transformed the way we understand the natural world with ideas that, in his day, were nothing short of revolutionary. He gave us insight into the fantastic diversity of life on Earth and its origins, including our own as a species.

Darwin's theory of evolution remains fundamental to the study of the natural world. Darwin changed not only the way we see all organisms, but also the way we see ourselves.

The most significant milestones in the life of Charles Darwin

  • 1809

    Born in Shrewsbury, Shropshire, Charles Robert Darwin was fascinated by the natural world from a young age. Growing up he was an avid reader of nature books and devoted his spare time to exploring the fields and woodlands around his home, collecting plants and insects.

  • 1825

    Charles enrolled in medical school at the University of Edinburgh, but he found lectures dull and surgery distressing, so he proved to be more interested in natural history than in his regular studies.

  • 1831

    Darwin accepted an offer to embark on a five-year voyage aboard HMS Beagle. The journey would change both his life and the trajectory of Western scientific thinking. On his travels Darwin collected plants, animals and took copious field notes, which provided the evidence he needed to develop his remarkable theory.

  • 1836

    He returned to England. A highly methodical scholar, Darwin spent many years comparing and analysing specimens before finally declaring that evolution occurs by a process of natural selection.

  • 1838

    After several rejections, Darwin finally accepted the post of Secretary of the Geological Society. A Few months later he was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society.

  • 1859

    Darwin published his book "On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection". To this day the theory of evolution by natural selection is accepted by the scientific community as the best evidence-based explanation for the diversity and complexity of life on Earth.

  • 1871

    The second Darwin's book on the evolutionary theory was out. "The Descent of Man, and Selection in Relation to Sex" fuelled even greater debate as it suggested that humans descended from apes.

  • 1882

    Darwin was diagnosed with a disease of the heart and passed away at Down House on 19 April. He was honoured by burial in Westminster Abbey, close to John Herschel and Isaac Newton.
